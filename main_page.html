Redfish Lake & the Sawtooths

Most of the hiking that I’ve done with my family has been around the beautiful Redfish Lake in Idaho.

Want to jump straight to the lake? Go to Redfish Lake ↓

Why Idaho Hiking Feels Different

Hiking in Idaho feels a little wild but still welcoming. Around Redfish Lake and the Sawtooth Mountains, the trails feel big and open, but you can still find quiet spots to sit by the water or watch the sun hit the peaks.

On this page I’m sharing some of my favorite hikes, photos, and tips for planning a trip to this special part of Idaho.

Types of Hikes I’ve Done in the Sawtooths

Here are a few of the different hikes I’ve done around Redfish Lake and the Sawtooths:

  1. Cramer Lakes Trail
    • About 13 miles round trip.
    • It took us a couple of days to complete. It was pretty aggressive with the incline and rocky sections, but it was beautiful to stay overnight by the lakes.
  2. Alpine and Baron Lakes
    • This one is more aggressive, around 26 miles round trip, and is usually a solid 3–4 day trip.
    • Even with the extra miles, it let us get deeper into the Idaho mountains and see amazing clear blue lakes and ponds.
  3. Redfish to Pettit Traverse
    • This is a real “beast” of a hike, hitting around 36 miles from one point to the other.
    • There are steeper climbs with big payoff views. Some sections take you along mountain ledges, and other parts dip down close to several lakes.
    • This one definitely takes around 4–5 days to complete and is not for the faint of heart.

No matter the level, I’ve learned that checking the forecast and knowing when the sun sets is just as important as having good shoes. Shoes are very important!

Redfish Lake

Redfish Lake is where I’ve spent most of my hiking time. I love starting near the lodge, following the shoreline, and watching the Sawtooth peaks reflect in the water. This is usually where my family and I start our adventures into the Sawtooths.

The lake is named for the red-colored sockeye salmon that return each year to spawn. It’s a popular spot for fishing, boating, and camping, but there are also plenty of trails that head into the surrounding mountains.

How I Plan a Sawtooth Hike

My basic planning checklist:

  • Check weather and trail conditions near Stanley, Idaho.
  • Make sure someone knows my route and return time.
  • Pack layers – mornings by the lake can be cold.
  • Pack enough water and food, including snacks for the hike.
  • Know when sunset is and bring a headlamp just in case.
  • Wear sturdy hiking boots or shoes with good traction.
